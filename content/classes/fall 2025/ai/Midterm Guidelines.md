## Summary

Create a work that is primarily visual in nature, and intersects with AI image-making (or analyzing) tools in some way. Your work can incorporate stills or moving image in whatever way makes sense, and be presented in a way best suited to the work (screening, prints, a website, an interactive experience, a video installation, projection mapping, etc.).

## Context

What ways can you take advantage of the limits of a tool? For example, consider the ["The Hardest Part" music video by Washed Out](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-Nb-M1GAOX8) that Hito Steyerl analyzes in *Medium Hot.* She notes that the filmmakers likely made the choice to depict characters running from behind as a way to **hide** the imperfections common in AI-generated video; to make the video look as realistic as possible. This, we might say, **is a creative aesthetic decision made to hide, or diminish a technical limitation.** What if instead we asked, **what is a creative aesthetic decision that exposes, or amplifies a technical limitation?**
![[Pasted image 20250918115449.png]]

Ultimately, the midterm "prompt" is conceptually open-ended: you are free to make whatever you want as long as your work engages materially with an AI technology that is related to images. Consider making something that is *only possible with AI.*

**Considerations:**
1. Your work doesn't have to be "about" AI specifically, but in what ways is your work in dialog with AI as a material?
2. What is the **context** of your work? As artists, it is important to *contextualize* our work. It's not just about the image you make, but where that image lives, how people confront it, or experience it, who can see it, etcetera. This context is particularly important when working with AI-generated output: if a machine is largely responsible for the visual output, **how can we contextualize that output to make it interesting?** What happens when you view "AI slop" designed for your Instagram Reels feed projected on a big screen?
3. Your work doesn't have to be "about" anything specific! Take this midterm as an opportunity to experiment, and go in weird directions with these tools; consider your role not as a creator, but as an **explorer**, or AI archaeologist, uncovering something hidden in the latent space. What strangeness might you stumble upon? If you go this route, it's your job explore the weirdness in its fullness, and then add context to it after the fact. What can you communicate to an audience about what you made, if you didn't have a strong plan going in?

## Guidelines
1. Whatever you make for this project, **you must present it with intention!** Don't just give us a PDF of your image outputs; think about how the context or framing of your images.
2. You don't have to use any of the specific tools I introduced in class. In fact, you are *encouraged* to work with a tool I have not covered or introduced in class!

## Proposal + proposal presentation guidelines
**Due next week, 9/25**

For your midterm proposal, you will give a brief 5-7 minute presentation to myself and the class. Create a short presentation that gives us an idea of the following:
1. what **tools** are you thinking of you using?
2. what **medium** are you thinking of working with mostly (image, video, installation, print, etc.)
3. how do you plan to **approach** using the tools?
4. what tools or processes are you curious about?
5. how are you considering presenting your work (you may not know until you get started, but give us an idea!)

You can make a slideshow, or a Milanote board, or an are.na channel, or something else, whatever you need to communicate your idea. it doesn't have to be super formal or exact, just enough for us to get what you're thinking about.
